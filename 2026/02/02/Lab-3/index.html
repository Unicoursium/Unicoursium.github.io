Method

The Bulb Board was driven using a DC voltage generated by the DAC on the PyBench board and controlled via MATLAB.
For each input voltage xdc​, the system was allowed to settle for 0.5 s to ensure that transient effects had decayed and that the output had reached steady state.

The output voltage ydc​, corresponding to the light intensity measured by the phototransistor and amplifier, was then sampled multiple times and averaged to reduce noise.
This procedure was repeated over a range of input voltages between 0 V and approximately 2.5 V, and the resulting DC input–output characteristic was plotted.

Results

The measured DC input–output characteristic is shown in Figure 1.

  • For low input voltages (below approximately 0.5), the output voltage remains close to zero and the light bulb does not emit visible light.

  • In the mid-range of input voltage (approximately 0.7-1.6), the output increases rapidly with input voltage.

  • At higher input voltages (above approximately 1.6V), the output voltage saturates at around 2.1V, and further increases in input voltage produce little change in output.

Task 3: Measure the Frequency Response of the Bulb Board system using PyBench

Method

To approximate linear system behaviour, the Bulb Board was operated within a small-signal region identified from the DC characteristic in Task 1.
The input signal was constrained to oscillate between 1.45 V and 1.55 V, ensuring that the system operated within an approximately linear region.

A sinusoidal input signal was generated using the DAC on the PyBench board and controlled via MATLAB.
For each test frequency, the output voltage corresponding to the measured light intensity was sampled at a sampling frequency of 100 Hz, and a sufficient number of samples were collected to capture steady-state behaviour.

The system gain at each frequency was calculated using the ratio of the output peak-to-peak voltage to the input peak-to-peak voltage:

∣G(jω)∣=xpp​ypp​​

The gain was then converted to decibels using:

GdB​=20log10​∣G(jω)∣

Measurements were initially performed at selected frequencies (1 Hz, 3 Hz, 4 Hz, 5 Hz, 7 Hz and 9 Hz), and subsequently automated to obtain measurements over the frequency range 1–20 Hz in steps of 1 Hz.

Results

The measured frequency response shows the following trends:

  • At low frequencies, the measured gain remains approximately constant, indicating that the output closely follows the input signal.

  • A peak in gain is observed at approximately 4hz and 5hz, corresponding to resonant behaviour of the system.

  • At higher frequencies, the gain decreases rapidly, indicating significant attenuation of high-frequency input signals.

When compared with the theoretical frequency response obtained in Task 2, the overall shape of the measured response is similar, including the presence of a resonant peak and high-frequency roll-off. However, discrepancies in gain magnitude and peak sharpness are observed.

Task 3a

Method

An automated MATLAB script was developed to measure the system gain at frequencies ranging from 1 Hz to 20 Hz in steps of 1 Hz.
At each frequency, a small-amplitude sinusoidal input signal was applied, with the operating point constrained between 1.45 V and 1.55 V to ensure approximately linear system behaviour.

For each frequency, the output signal was sampled at a fixed sampling frequency and the steady-state portion of the signal was analysed.
The system gain was calculated using the ratio of output to input peak-to-peak amplitudes and converted to decibels.
The measured gain values were then plotted as a function of frequency to obtain the experimental frequency response of the system.

Results

The measured frequency response obtained from the automated sweep shows a clear resonant peak and a gradual roll-off at higher frequencies.
The overall shape of the experimental frequency response closely resembles the theoretical prediction from Task 2.

However, differences are observed in the magnitude of the gain and the sharpness of the resonant peak. In particular:

  • The measured peak gain is lower than the theoretical value.

  • The resonant peak appears broader and less pronounced than predicted.

  • High-frequency attenuation is stronger in the measured response.

Task 4: Step Response of the Bulb Board

Method

The Bulb Board was driven using step changes in input voltage generated by the DAC on the PyBench board and controlled via MATLAB.
A rising step from 0.7 V to 1.2 V was first applied, followed by a falling step from 1.2 V back to 0.7 V.

The output voltage, corresponding to the measured light intensity, was sampled at a fixed sampling frequency, and the transient response was recorded over a sufficient duration to capture the full system response, including oscillations and settling behaviour.

Results

The measured step response exhibits clear transient dynamics.

Following the rising step input, the output voltage increases rapidly and overshoots the final steady-state value before undergoing oscillations that gradually decay with time.
A similar oscillatory behaviour is observed following the falling step, although the amplitude and shape of the response differ slightly due to the asymmetric behaviour of the physical system.

After the transient oscillations decay, the output settles to a steady-state value consistent with the DC characteristic obtained in Task 1.
